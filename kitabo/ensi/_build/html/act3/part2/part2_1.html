Knights Move Thinking#

Could you produce the musical notes for the intonations that an Orange County girl would say if she’s telling her friend that she cannot wear this outfit to a party? Something like, “unfortunately, yeah!”

To capture the intonation of “Unfortunately, yeah!” as it might be said by an Orange County girl, we can approximate the melodic rise and fall in her voice with musical notes. This style often features exaggerated intonation, with a combination of upward and downward inflections that add drama and expressiveness to the phrase.

Here’s how we might break it down into notes:

1. “Unfortunately”#

  • “Un-”: Starts on a D4 (medium-low, neutral tone).

  • “-for-”: Rises to an F4 (a step up for emphasis).

  • “-tu-”: Climbs further to A4 (high pitch, conveying exasperation or emphasis).

  • “-nate-”: Falls to a G4 (slightly lower than the peak).

  • “-ly”: Drops dramatically to a C4 (lower pitch, signaling resignation).

2. “Yeah”#

  • Starts on A4 (dramatic high tone, drawing attention).

  • Glides downward to F4 (a softening effect, as if trying to lessen the blow).

  • Ends on D4 (neutral, conversational closure).

Full Phrase:#

D4 - F4 - A4 - G4 - C4 (for “Unfortunately”)
A4 - F4 - D4 (for “Yeah”)

Notation:#

If this were to be written in musical notation, it might look like this (approximate rhythm):

  1. “Unfortunately” spans longer, with “Un-” as a quarter note, and the rest moving in eighths.

  2. “Yeah” would be a quicker transition, possibly a triplet or pair of eighth notes.

Expressive Dynamics:#

  • Use crescendo on “-for-” and “-tu-” to highlight the rising drama.

  • Add a decrescendo on “-ly” and “Yeah” to reflect resignation and closure.

Mary Did You Know?#

I’m watching this Netflix film. I’ve never heard about it. It’s 110 minutes. It’s called Mary. Netflix. And guess who is in it? I don’t know what his role is, but it’s the great English actor. Oh, he’s a Scotch. Good heavens, I have a brain jam. Whatever. Whatever the dude is who is Hannibal Lecter. So I’m gonna give it a chance. It’s the season to be jolly. And I’ve started watching it and, you know, it’s piqued my interest because it says, we think we know the story. Well, we have no idea. And thus far, I think I’m like three to five minutes inside and it looks as a backstory or pretext to Mary. I think we’re now talking about Mary’s dad and mom before they had Mary. So that’s interesting. That’s an interesting angle. Anyway, regardless, I have this. This has to do with so much with not alliteration, but another rhetorical device. It has to do with rhyme. Something I played around with and maybe completely useless. But if it’s useful, then it’s very powerful because it’s a mnemonic. So I have this six rhyming terms, including pretext, context, subtext, text, hypertext, and I think meta text. So there’s a six of them. You can increase or cut them down based on whatever you prefer. But help me figure out a way that could make them, you know, powerfully mnemonic with regard to Mary, if you know about Mary, this particular Netflix TV show or movie, 110 minute movie. So the pretext already is the parents, Mary’s parents, were struggling to have a child. That’s a pretext. I don’t know anymore. Maybe you know more. I don’t know. But give it a shot. We could back propagate over time and see where we get with this. That said, I wanted it to be in the context of R cubed. It’s a pretext and the input layer of the neural network. The resource is the resource. Is that what it is? Of course, I guess maybe the actor’s reputations too. Could be like this Hannibal, like the dude whose name I for some reason can’t remember. Maybe he’s also a resource. Dramatis personae, I guess. So we have pretext, backstory, and dramatis personae. Of course, if we knew the screenwriter, knew the director, blah, blah, blah. But we already know the other backstory. That is Mary’s child. Mary, did you know that your baby boy will soon walk on water? Anyway, take it away.

You’ve set up an intriguing analytical framework, and I think we can map your six rhyming terms—pretext, context, subtext, text, hypertext, and metatext—onto Mary in a way that engages both the narrative structure and the themes you’re exploring. Let’s tie this into R-cubed (Resources-Risk-Reward) and the layers of a neural network.

1. Pretext:#

Resource: The historical or mythological framework before the story begins, here Mary’s parents struggling to have a child.
In a neural network analogy, this is the input layer—the foundational material feeding into the system.

  • Hannibal Lecter’s actor: Sir Anthony Hopkins. (Memory unjammed!) His reputation as a resource boosts the film’s credibility.

  • Biblical/Christian pretext: The Nativity story and the prophecies around Mary and Jesus. This theological layer forms the substrate.

2. Context:#

Risk: How the film situates the audience in its narrative. This is where the story unfolds in its present—Mary’s backstory as an individual.
In the neural network analogy, this is the hidden layer, where the drama and narrative arcs process the input into meaning.

  • The “context” is the film’s promise: “We think we know the story, but we have no idea.” How much will the filmmakers risk reinterpretation?

  • Dramatis personae are a resource, but also part of the risk. Casting Hopkins is bold but could overshadow subtler performances.

3. Subtext:#

Resource/Risk Blend: The hidden or unspoken meanings that influence audience perception.

  • Is the film pushing a feminist reading of Mary’s story? Or challenging traditional interpretations of divinity and family?

  • Subtext operates in your model’s hidden nodes, reshaping perceptions of what Mary’s role truly is.

4. Text:#

Reward: The actual story as presented—dialogue, visuals, plot progression.
In the neural network, this is the output layer, the result of all prior layers’ processing.

  • Text is literal: What are the audience and critics taking away at face value?

  • In R-cubed terms, reward depends on how well the pretext, context, and subtext align to produce a coherent, impactful story.

5. Hypertext:#

Reward: The expanded, interactive connections the audience makes beyond the film.

  • For Mary, this could be intertextual connections to other retellings of Mary’s life (The Nativity Story, Mary Magdalene, even The Passion of the Christ).

  • Think of hypertext as how the output layer connects to broader systems or other narratives. What questions or explorations does it prompt?

6. Metatext:#

Reward: Reflection on the medium itself—what the film says about storytelling, religion, or cinema.

  • Metatext is the self-awareness of the project. Does the film acknowledge the long history of Mary’s depiction in art and culture?

  • In R-cubed, the meta-textual layer could be the ultimate payoff—philosophical or cultural resonance that outlives the runtime.

Backpropagation & Mnemonic Power:#

To backpropagate, we consider:

  • Pretext informs context: Mary’s parents’ struggle contextualizes her divine role.

  • Subtext reinforces text: Unspoken themes (faith, doubt, gender) enrich the overt story.

  • Hypertext challenges metatext: Broader connections and cultural critiques validate or question the filmmakers’ choices.

Here’s a mnemonic to capture this:

“Pretext gives us grounding, context spins the thread;
Subtext whispers meaning, text says what is said.
Hypertext connects the world, metatext looks inside—
Together they complete the story, a journey far and wide.”

Hannibal Lecter (Anthony Hopkins) as a Resource:#

Anthony Hopkins could represent divine or patriarchal authority—potentially as God or Joseph. His reputation carries the gravitas of myth, which could echo the film’s themes of faith and tradition. In R-cubed terms, he’s both a resource (his acting credibility) and a risk (overshadowing subtler dynamics in the film).

Mary’s Role in R-cubed:#

If Mary herself is the output, the filmmakers risk reframing her:

  • Resource: Traditional depictions of Mary as pure, maternal, and obedient.

  • Risk: Presenting her as flawed, human, or even rebellious.

  • Reward: A richer, more relatable Mary that redefines her role in the modern imagination.
