Transforming Available Resources#

TARLSEA

Transforming Available Resources through Luck, Skill, Education, and into Agency

This acronym provides a succinct summary of the essence of a neural network’s functionality, blending the metaphorical with the practical:

  1. Transforming Available Resources (TAR): Neural networks take input data, which can be seen as raw, untapped resources, and apply transformations through layers of computation to extract meaningful patterns and insights.

  2. Luck (L): In a stochastic sense, randomness plays a role in initializing weights, introducing variety and adaptability. It also reflects the serendipity of finding the right combinations through iterative processes like backpropagation.

  3. Skill (S): The trained network embodies skill, honed through learning from data and fine-tuning weights to optimize performance. This is analogous to acquiring expertise through practice.

  4. Education (E): The training process is a form of education, where the network learns to generalize from examples and refine its understanding of underlying patterns.

  5. Agency (A): The output layer represents agency, where the network acts upon its learning to make predictions, decisions, or classifications, effectively transforming inputs into actionable insights.

By combining these elements, TARLSEA captures the journey from raw data to empowered action, emphasizing the interplay of chance, refinement, and structured learning in achieving purposeful outcomes. It also resonates metaphorically with human processes of growth and agency.

Cool V7 Voice Leading#

What is one of the coolest voice leads for the Dominant 7?

One of the coolest voice leadings for a Dominant 7 (V7) chord involves chromatic inner voice motion—a hallmark of rich harmonic movement. Here’s an example:

Voice Leading: V7 to I or vi (Resolution)#

Chord Progression:

  • V7 (G7): G - B - D - F

  • I (C): C - E - G (or sometimes C - E - G - B for a jazzier major 7th)

Cool Voice Leading Option:#

  1. Keep G in the bass.

  2. Let the B (3rd) resolve upward chromatically to C (root of the tonic).

  3. Let the F (7th) resolve downward chromatically to E (3rd of the tonic).

  4. Keep the D (5th) static (common tone) or resolve it to C (root) if doubling is desired.

Chromatic Tension to the vi (Deceptive Cadence):#

Instead of resolving to the tonic (I), resolve the V7 to vi (Am). This creates a more dramatic resolution, often used in classical and jazz contexts.

  • V7 (G7): G - B - D - F

  • vi (Am): A - C - E

Voice Leading:#

  1. G moves stepwise down to A.

  2. B resolves stepwise up to C.

  3. D moves stepwise down to E.

  4. F resolves stepwise downward to E (or upward chromatically to G# in jazz contexts, adding extra color).

Why It’s Cool:#

  1. The chromatic inner voice motion (F to E, B to C) provides tension and smoothness simultaneously.

  2. It leverages common tones (like D in the first example) to anchor the listener’s ear.

  3. The deceptive cadence (to vi) surprises without sounding jarring.

  4. In jazz or gospel, adding alterations like F♯ (as a 13th) or resolving F to G# creates stunning expressive possibilities.

Why did Nietzsche think of the English?

Nietzsche had a complicated, often scathing view of the English, which he articulated in many of his works, particularly Beyond Good and Evil and On the Genealogy of Morality. He associated the English with qualities he found deeply distasteful—pragmatism, utilitarianism, and a penchant for mediocrity disguised as morality. Let me unpack his critique of the English from several angles:

1. The English Morality of Mediocrity#

Nietzsche criticized the English for their focus on utilitarianism, a philosophy he associated with figures like John Stuart Mill and Jeremy Bentham. He dismissed the idea of the “greatest happiness for the greatest number” as a slave morality, a leveling ideology that prioritized comfort, equality, and herd instincts over excellence, individuality, and the will to power. Nietzsche saw this morality as anti-life because it sought to eliminate struggle and risk—the very forces that drive growth and greatness.

2. Hostility Toward English Empiricism#

Nietzsche had little patience for the empiricist tradition of thinkers like Locke, Hume, and even Darwin. While he acknowledged Darwin’s influence, he felt Darwinism reduced life to mere survival and adaptation rather than embracing the creative, dynamic forces that Nietzsche associated with the will to power. English empiricism, to Nietzsche, was narrow-minded, overly reliant on facts and observable phenomena, and devoid of the kind of grand, speculative philosophy he admired in the Germans, French, and Greeks.

3. The Problem of the English Spirit#

Nietzsche often derided what he perceived as the banality of the English spirit. He thought the English lacked a deep cultural and artistic soul, contrasting them unfavorably with the Greeks, the Germans, and even the French. He described the English as “shopkeepers” whose worldview was dominated by materialism, commerce, and a pragmatic dullness that stifled the higher aspirations of humanity.

4. English Hypocrisy and Christianity#

Nietzsche saw the English as particularly hypocritical in their embrace of Christian morality. While he considered Christianity a form of slave morality in general, he thought the English perfected its fusion with bourgeois values like industriousness, thrift, and self-righteousness. He viewed the English moral framework as a tool for justifying exploitation and colonial domination while maintaining a veneer of righteousness.

5. Exception: Shakespeare#

Interestingly, Nietzsche made an exception for Shakespeare, whom he admired as a profound and tragic artist. Shakespeare, to Nietzsche, transcended the mediocrity of English culture by grappling with the raw, existential forces of life. In him, Nietzsche saw a kindred spirit who delved into the depths of human nature, far removed from the pedestrian morality Nietzsche detested.

6. Ambivalence Toward English Power#

Nietzsche recognized England’s geopolitical dominance and cultural reach during his time but viewed it as an expression of mediocrity rather than greatness. England’s power, he felt, was based on control, colonization, and commerce—not the higher values of art, philosophy, or spiritual depth. This echoes your observation of England as a nation built on exploitation rather than intellectual creation.

Conclusion#

Nietzsche thought of the English as the epitome of slave morality and mediocrity disguised as virtue. He saw them as practical and powerful but devoid of the higher spiritual and cultural aspirations he valued. Yet, in figures like Shakespeare, Nietzsche glimpsed exceptions—proof that even within a culture he criticized so harshly, there were moments of transcendent greatness.

Your analysis of England’s contributions in the arts is both incisive and provocative, though perhaps a bit colored by an adversarial stance toward English cultural output. While I broadly agree with the overall distinction you make between England and the European continent in terms of artistic achievements, I think there are nuances worth exploring.

Literature: England’s Singular Triumph#

Shakespeare’s place as the towering figure of literature is undisputed, and the King James Bible is an unparalleled work of literary craftsmanship. England’s poetry—Donne, Milton, Wordsworth, and T.S. Eliot (though American-born)—cements its literary dominance. However, the singularity of this triumph contrasts sharply with the breadth of contributions from the continent. France alone offers Rabelais, Molière, Voltaire, Balzac, Flaubert, Proust, and Camus, not to mention the Russian literary juggernauts—Tolstoy, Dostoyevsky, and Chekhov—all of whom expand literature beyond even Shakespearean boundaries in terms of scope and philosophy. England shines with one blinding star, but the continent gives us constellations.

Music: The Deafening Silence of England#

Here, your critique feels justified. Aside from Purcell and the later contributions of Elgar, Vaughan Williams, and Britten, England’s contributions to classical music pale in comparison to Germany, Austria, and Italy. The Baroque, Classical, and Romantic periods are overwhelmingly continental in character. Handel, though often claimed by England, remains fundamentally German. England’s musical landscape seems more tied to folk traditions and modern popular music (e.g., The Beatles and Pink Floyd), which, while culturally seismic, do not belong to the classical canon.

Visual Arts: A Struggle to Keep Up#

The Pre-Raphaelites are charming but provincial compared to the continental titans of the Renaissance and the Impressionist and Post-Impressionist movements. England simply does not produce a Michelangelo, Titian, or Van Gogh. Even in the 20th century, movements like Cubism, Surrealism, and Abstract Expressionism bypass England almost entirely. Turner and Constable are exceptions, but they stand more as exceptional painters rather than representatives of a larger movement.

Opera and Theatre: Italy’s and France’s Playground#

You’re right to highlight opera as an Italian-dominated art form, with England offering almost nothing comparable. Even in theatre, England’s Renaissance brilliance (Marlowe, Shakespeare) dwindles into mediocrity post-Restoration, whereas French theatre continues with Racine, Molière, and later Beckett, and German theatre produces Goethe, Schiller, and Brecht.

Religious Arts and Hymnody: England’s Quiet Innovation#

Your observation about the hymnal tradition is fascinating. The Anglican hymnal tradition, combining literary elegance with musical accessibility, did catalyze a Protestant aesthetic distinct from Catholic traditions. It’s an unsung achievement (no pun intended) that deserves more recognition, particularly as it influenced the Protestant cultural dominance in North America.

Culinary Arts: The Silent Revolution#

Here I diverge slightly. England’s culinary reputation, historically mocked, has undergone a renaissance in recent decades. The resurgence of traditional ingredients, combined with influences from India and other colonies, has revitalized British cuisine. Still, the continent—with its wines, cheeses, and gastronomic heritage—remains unmatched in diversity and prestige.

Football: A Point of National Embarrassment#

The Premier League’s global dominance is fueled by foreign talent and money, not homegrown brilliance. Spain, Brazil, Italy, and Germany dominate in international footballing achievements. Your point about the 1966 World Cup is contentious but resonates with those who see that year as more an aberration than a testament to English footballing prowess.

England as a Case of Specialization#

If we step back, it becomes clear that England excels in specific areas—literature, certain architectural styles (Gothic revival), and, more recently, popular culture (music, film)—while the continent overwhelms in breadth. England’s strengths lie in discrete, monumental achievements, while Europe’s contributions are more holistic, giving rise to sustained movements across multiple domains.

In essence, England is a cultural specialist, while Europe is a polymath. England produces Shakespeare, the Gothic cathedral, and the Industrial Revolution, but the continent gives us the Renaissance, the Baroque, and Romanticism. It’s a trade-off, one that makes both essential to the larger story of Western civilization.
