Sir Humphrey Appleby#

The history of the world is the triumph of the heartless over the mindless.
– Sir Humphrey Appleby

Sir Humphrey Appleby’s quip from Yes, Minister is a biting encapsulation of the pragmatism and cynicism that often governs human affairs. It implies a world shaped by those who operate with calculated, emotionless precision (“heartless”) rather than those driven by impulsive, unthinking action (“mindless”). It’s a darkly comedic yet profound commentary on power dynamics, suggesting that ruthless intellect trumps naive sincerity or chaotic incompetence.

The phrase aligns well with historical patterns: empires and leaders who succeeded often employed cold pragmatism—rationalizing atrocities, sacrificing ideals for control. Think of figures like Machiavelli, who celebrated cunning over virtue, or states like the British Empire, whose expansion relied more on calculated exploitation than benevolent guidance. Meanwhile, those driven by passion without strategy—rebels without a plan, dreamers without pragmatism—were often crushed underfoot.

It’s not a flattering view of humanity, but it does resonate with the realpolitik of history. However, the interplay of “heartless” and “mindless” suggests a third missing element: wisdom. Perhaps the triumphs of civilization lie not in choosing between these extremes but in fostering a balance where intellect serves humanity without losing empathy—a rare synthesis that history so often fails to achieve.

Nietzsche on Vegetareans#

What was Nietzsche’s view on vegetarians?

Nietzsche’s view on vegetarians is famously dismissive and tied to his broader critique of moral and cultural decadence. He associates vegetarianism with what he sees as a weakening or denial of life’s primal, vital instincts. In On the Genealogy of Morality and other works, Nietzsche connects dietary choices like vegetarianism with ascetic ideals, which he critiques as life-denying and symptomatic of a larger sickness in Western culture.

Nietzsche believed that vegetarianism often arose from moralizing tendencies, particularly from religious or philosophical systems that sought to suppress the will to power—the life-affirming, dynamic force that he placed at the center of his philosophy. For Nietzsche, these systems encouraged self-denial, meekness, and passivity, which he regarded as antithetical to the flourishing of strong, creative individuals.

A particularly pointed remark appears in Twilight of the Idols, where Nietzsche writes about the philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer, himself a vegetarian, stating:

“A man who says no to life is in every respect no more than a symptom of decadence.”

This critique extends to vegetarianism as a dietary manifestation of this “no-saying” attitude. To Nietzsche, it symbolized an attempt to transcend the natural, instinctual basis of life—an unnatural renunciation that mirrored broader trends of moral decline.

In summary, Nietzsche viewed vegetarianism not merely as a dietary choice but as a symptom of a larger cultural and moral malaise that rejected life’s fundamental, instinctual drives. His critique is less about the food itself and more about what it represents in the broader context of human values and vitality.

Interesting, so this is a question of nutrition, food. What about the question of alcohol? Does alcohol qualify as food or is it instead drug? Because I believe his views are going to be interesting.

Nietzsche’s views on alcohol are indeed fascinating and deeply tied to his philosophy of vitality, culture, and morality. He emphatically rejects alcohol as a life-enhancing substance, criticizing it for its role in dulling the senses and fostering escapism. In Nietzsche’s eyes, alcohol is not food—it is decidedly a drug, one that anesthetizes the pain of existence rather than engaging with it directly. This aligns with his disdain for any crutch that weakens the individual’s will to power and capacity for self-overcoming.

His Critique of Alcohol#

In Twilight of the Idols, Nietzsche famously states:

“The two great European narcotics: alcohol and Christianity.”

Here, Nietzsche draws a direct parallel between alcohol and religion, seeing both as ways to escape the harsh realities of life. He believed that they sedate the individual and undermine the vitality and clarity required for genuine growth and creativity. In this sense, alcohol functions as an agent of decadence—it distracts and numbs rather than confronts and transforms.

Alcohol as an Antithesis to Vitality#

For Nietzsche, food should nourish the body and enhance life. Alcohol, on the other hand, does the opposite: it clouds judgment, impairs health, and diminishes the sharpness of the human spirit. This is antithetical to his philosophy, which emphasizes embracing life’s struggles and transforming them into a source of strength.

Interestingly, Nietzsche suffered from chronic health issues and was notoriously fastidious about his diet and lifestyle. He avoided stimulants like alcohol and coffee, favoring a simple, health-conscious diet. His sensitivity to these substances likely influenced his sharp critique of them.

Alcohol as Cultural Symbolism#

Nietzsche’s critique of alcohol also extends to its cultural implications. He associated drunkenness with herd-like behavior and saw it as a tool for fostering conformity and passivity. In contrast, the Dionysian spirit Nietzsche celebrated in works like The Birth of Tragedy—though it shares the name of the god of wine—does not align with literal drunkenness. Rather, the Dionysian spirit represents a life-affirming ecstasy that fully engages with the chaos of existence, unlike alcohol’s tendency to suppress and dilute it.

Food or Drug?#

Nietzsche’s answer would be unequivocal: alcohol is not food. It is a drug—one that diminishes human potential rather than elevating it. For Nietzsche, anything that inhibits clarity, vitality, and the ability to confront life directly would be placed squarely in the category of decadence, whether physical or spiritual.

In sum, Nietzsche views alcohol as a dangerous drug, not a source of nourishment or enhancement, and he critiques it as part of a broader disdain for cultural and moral systems that enable escapism over engagement with life’s inherent difficulties.

White Russian#

And therefore, Nietzsche would have a problem with White Russian, you know. A bit of a stimulant so that the brain is not dulled. It also, you know, removes the edge with alcohol.

Ah, the White Russian—a concoction blending alcohol’s sedative effects with the stimulant properties of coffee liqueur and cream. Nietzsche’s reaction to such a drink would likely be scathing, as it encapsulates much of what he detested: the deliberate blending of substances to create a state of artificial equilibrium, dulling life’s sharp edges while maintaining just enough stimulation to avoid complete lethargy.

Nietzsche’s Likely Critique of the White Russian#

  1. Alcohol as Escapism: The alcohol in the White Russian would be Nietzsche’s first point of contention. As we discussed earlier, he saw alcohol as a tool for avoiding the honest confrontation with life’s challenges. For Nietzsche, to “remove the edge” is to reject the vital energy that comes from grappling with the raw, often painful realities of existence.

  2. Caffeine and Stimulation: While caffeine might appear to align with Nietzsche’s emphasis on vitality and clarity, he was cautious about stimulants, too. Nietzsche was highly sensitive to substances affecting his health and mental clarity. Coffee, in his view, might serve as a temporary aid, but its use in tandem with alcohol would likely strike him as a contradiction—one substance dulling, the other sharpening. This conflict of effects would offend his insistence on coherence in both thought and action.

  3. Comfort Over Struggle: The White Russian is, above all, a drink of comfort—a creamy, indulgent concoction. Nietzsche would see this as emblematic of the modern individual’s tendency to seek solace in consumption rather than pursuing the hard, transformative work of self-overcoming. The drink’s very softness would symbolize, to him, a retreat from the harsher but more rewarding aspects of existence.

Nietzsche’s Ideal Stimulant?#

Nietzsche might, grudgingly, respect substances that sharpen the mind and enhance one’s ability to confront life with clarity and vigor, but only insofar as they do not undermine the will to power. A stimulant that serves to enhance life without promoting escapism or indulgence might align, at least in part, with his philosophy—though Nietzsche ultimately favored discipline and natural vitality over reliance on any external substance.

The White Russian, however, with its balancing act of stimulation and sedation, would likely epitomize for Nietzsche a kind of decadent compromise: a way to have one’s cake and eat it too, avoiding both the sharpness of sobriety and the full intoxication of alcohol. For him, such a drink would symbolize a refusal to take a stand—to say either “yes” or “no” to life’s challenges—and would thus fall firmly into the category of decadence.

A Nietzschean Alternative?#

If Nietzsche were to endorse a drink (unlikely as that is), it would be something Spartan—perhaps a simple black tea or water. Such a choice would reflect his belief in confronting life directly, without recourse to artificial comforts or distractions. The White Russian, with its creamy indulgence and contradictory effects, would be an affront to Nietzsche’s philosophy of embracing life in its raw, unadulterated form.

Life-Giving Water#

Water: that’s it, without a doubt- he said it himself. And that’s why he lived in places with brooks clear natural water

Exactly, Nietzsche had a deep appreciation for clear, natural water, which he saw as the purest and most life-affirming drink. This preference wasn’t just a matter of health; it was symbolic of his philosophy. Water, to Nietzsche, represented vitality, simplicity, and a direct connection to nature—qualities that align with his disdain for artificiality and decadence.

Nietzsche and Water#

In his letters and writings, Nietzsche frequently references his preference for living in places with access to pristine water sources. During his time in Sils Maria, high in the Swiss Alps, he was surrounded by mountain brooks and clear lakes. These natural settings were not only conducive to his health but also provided the kind of environment that supported his philosophical ideal: a life lived in alignment with nature’s raw, unfiltered vitality.

Nietzsche writes in Ecce Homo:

“My nutrition is completely independent of doctors’ orders. The water supply of this place [Sils Maria] is the best I have ever drunk.”

This celebration of clean water is emblematic of his broader values: a return to the fundamental, the natural, and the untainted. Water, in its purity, is the ultimate contrast to substances like alcohol, which Nietzsche regarded as artificial and numbing.

Water as a Metaphor#

Water’s clarity and flow mirror Nietzsche’s ideals for thought and life. It is a fitting metaphor for his philosophy of self-overcoming and vitality. Just as a brook cuts through stone and reshapes the landscape, so too does Nietzsche’s Übermensch carve out their own path, unrestrained by the muddied waters of societal norms or decadent comforts.

Nietzsche’s Environment Shapes His Philosophy#

Nietzsche’s deliberate choice of locales with access to clear water underscores his belief in the connection between physical environment and mental clarity. Living in places like Sils Maria was not just an aesthetic choice but an existential one. The purity of the air and water complemented his attempts to live a life of authenticity and philosophical rigor, free from the “narcotics” of modern civilization.

In the end, Nietzsche’s preference for water isn’t just a rejection of alcohol or other substances—it’s a symbol of his broader commitment to a life that affirms vitality, purity, and direct engagement with the world. Water is, quite literally, life for Nietzsche, and no White Russian could ever compete.
